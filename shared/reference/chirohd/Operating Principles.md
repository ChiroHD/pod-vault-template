## Overview

Personal operating principles for the PM role at ChiroHD. Synthesized from *Inspired* (Cagan), PM Bootcamp preparation, and ChiroHD-specific context. These are decision-making guides, not rigid rules.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. You Are Paid to Learn (First 90 Days)

Credibility comes from understanding, not shipping. Resist the urge to prove yourself by shipping fast. The most valuable thing you can do in month 1 is understand the business, customers, and technology deeply enough to make good decisions in months 2-12.

*Source: 30-60-90 Plan, Cagan*

### 2. Execute the Vision Before You Shape It

Gabe built ChiroHD from scratch. Dr. C is establishing the product org. Your job first is to understand and execute their vision, not impose your own. Earn the right to shape direction by demonstrating you understand the current one.

*Source: Field Guide Insight #2*

### 3. Engineers Are Innovation Partners, Not Ticket-Takers

Bring engineers into the problem space early. Share customer pain points, business context, and constraints — then collaborate on solutions. The best product ideas come from engineers who understand the "why."

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 14), Field Guide Insight #3*

### 4. Talk to Chiropractors Every Week

Customer contact is non-negotiable. Aim for weekly chiropractor conversations, even brief ones. You cannot make good product decisions from inside the building.

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 36), Field Guide Insight #4*

### 5. Validate Value, Not Usability

The biggest risk is building something nobody wants, not building something hard to use. Before polishing the UX, prove the value proposition. Use prototypes to test demand, not just design.

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 42), Field Guide Insight #6*

### 6. Show Prototypes, Not Presentations

Stakeholders react to what they can interact with, not what they read. Use prototypes (even Wizard of Oz) to communicate ideas. This is especially important with Gabe, who thinks visually and experientially.

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 31), Field Guide Insight #7*

### 7. Answer Four Questions Before Any Feature

Before committing engineering time to anything:
1. **Will customers buy/use this?** (Value risk)
2. **Can users figure it out?** (Usability risk)
3. **Can engineering build it?** (Feasibility risk)
4. **Does the business support it?** (Business viability risk)

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 24), Field Guide Insight #8*

### 8. Outcomes Over Output

Measure impact, not velocity. Celebrate customer outcomes, not features shipped. This requires establishing baseline metrics early and tracking them consistently.

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 20), Field Guide Insight #9*

### 9. Test the Biggest Risk First

Don't spend weeks building when the biggest risk can be tested in days. Identify the riskiest assumption in any initiative and design the fastest possible experiment to validate or kill it.

*Source: Cagan (Chapter 33), Field Guide Insight #10*

### 10. Add Structure Without Adding Bureaucracy

Gabe is process-averse. The PM function will only survive if it makes things faster and clearer, not slower and more complex. Every process you introduce must clearly earn its keep.

*Source: ChiroHD context (Gabe's style), Field Guide Insight #1*

---

## ChiroHD-Specific Principles

### 11. Respect the Founder's Creation

ChiroHD is Gabe's life work. Every suggestion should build on the foundation, not imply it was built wrong. Frame changes as evolution, not correction.

### 12. Domain Expertise Is a Superpower

Dr. C is a Doctor of Chiropractic. Leverage her clinical knowledge as a competitive advantage in product decisions. Generic PM frameworks + deep clinical insight = better products than competitors can build.

### 13. Earn Trust in Concentric Circles

Start with Dr. C (manager) → Patrick (engineering) → Luke (operations) → Gabe (founder) → Bashir (PE). Each relationship builds evidence for the next.

### 14. The AI Window Is Closing

Competitors are shipping AI features now. Every month without a ChiroHD AI strategy is market share risk. Balance "learn first" with "the market won't wait."

### 15. Write Things Down

In a growing org, undocumented decisions get relitigated. Document decisions, rationale, and context. This protects everyone and builds institutional knowledge.

---

## Decision-Making Heuristics

When stuck, ask:
- "What would make the chiropractor's day better?" → Customer focus
- "What would Gabe want to protect?" → Founder alignment
- "What can we test this week?" → Bias to action
- "What does the data say?" → Evidence over opinion
- "Is this adding structure or adding bureaucracy?" → Process discipline
